What is design thinking...

...and why is it so important?

Introduction

IDEO is a global design company committed to creating positive impact (IDEO at a Glance, 2019). They are a community with shared values and beliefs that influence the work they do, and the impact they hope to achieve. IDEO has ten locations worldwide and employs over 600 creatives. The IDEO group is a part of the Kyu Collective, a group of diverse creative companies committed to addressing the most significant challenges facing business and society.

IDEO considers themselves as early leaders in the practice of design thinking, creating positive impact through design by applying their creative mindsets and skills and teaching others to do the same. They help clients and customers around the world build the capacity and outcomes required to navigate today’s complexity and lead their markets.

IDEO U

IDEO U is an online school run by the IDEO group which aims to equip designers with the tools needed to stay adaptable and relevant when the ‘gold standard’ changes every day. IDEO U instructors are subject matter experts who have led thousands of projects across industries and around the globe. This experience allows them to apply their knowledge and insight to their teaching methods, letting designers have real-world examples on how their designs should work.

Design Thinking

The Interaction Design Foundation (Friis Dam and Yu Siang, 2020) defines design thinking as:

“...an iterative process in which we seek to understand the user, challenge assumptions, and redefine problems in an attempt to identify alternative strategies and solutions that might not be instantly apparent with our initial level of understanding.”

Design thinking provides a solution-based approach to solving problems. It is a hands-on combination of thought processes and creative approaches. The greatest part of design thinking isn’t the design you form at the end, but more the processes you use to reach your finished product.

IDEO U and Design Thinking

Understanding what design thinking is is not equal to being able to apply it. IDEO U IDEOU, (What is Design Thinking?, n.d.) aims to take the guessing out of the equation by offering 4 easy steps down the design-thinking path:

Method 1: “Practice Empathy, Observation, and Interviewing”

Get to know who your target user is. Paying attention to current user trends, researching accessibility and usability, knowing current design trends and seeking advice from actual users is the best way to ensure you’re designing in an effective way

Method 2: “Build Scrappy Prototypes”

IDEO stresses the importance of some of the most primitive design tools we have- pen and paper. When you design on paper, you are limited only by your imagination and could come up with an idea you don’t necessarily know how to recreate on screen. This will gently push you to learn more about the design softwares you choose to use without feeling so confined by your initial understanding of it.

Method 3: “Turn Problems into Questions”

Often, issues can feel unsolvable if a solution doesn’t immediately come to you. To combat this frustration, IDEO suggests asking questions which will help you get to the root of the issue, leaving you with a deeper understanding of the problem at hand and ultimately leading to a much better solution.

Method 4: “Use Research to Understand the Past, Present, and Future”

Generative research identifies new opportunities and needs. Evaluative research gathers feedback and helps you iterate forward. These types of research focus on the future, whereas Validating research looks at what is currently happening. Balancing your research approach to focus on what’s happening now and what could be is one of the most important skills to develop

Why is Design Thinking so Important?

WeWork Ideas (Tuttle, 2021) breaks the importance of design thinking into 4 points:

1. “Design Thinking aims to solve a concrete human need.”

Design thinking can allow teams to discover issues that consumers have that either the designer or consumer (or both) were not even aware existed. Once these issues are identified, solutions can be developed for them.

2.“Design Thinking tackles Issues that are Ambiguous or Difficult to Define.”

Designers cannot expect users to always know how to express the issues they are having. The consumer could know exactly what they dislike about a product, but have no idea how to communicate the issue to the relevant team. The design thinking process enables the designer to discover these difficult to define issues and allows creative solutions to odd problems.

3. “Design Thinking leads to more Innovative Solutions.”

Because of the interactive nature of the design thinking process, there is an environment created in which ideas can surface. The best way to innovate is to follow a process which allows you to explore non-obvious solutions- which is exactly what design thinking does.

4. “Design Thinking makes Organisations run Faster and more Efficiently.”

Taking the guessing out of the equation allows organisations to come up with creative solutions much more quickly than if they spent a greater amount of time trying to put ideas together in a more clinical and formal way than just materialising any substantial ideas and seeing which works best.

The Problems with Design Thinking

As with any creative process, design thinking is not a ‘perfect’ system- mainly because it is too easily understood. It seems backward to deem a system as ‘too accessible’, especially when accessibility is more of a concern than ever. However, organisations can latch onto design thinking as it is an accepted and easily interpreted concept, forcing design thinking to become a ‘buzzword’ used by designers who don’t necessarily understand how to actually implement it.

Customerthink.com (Kelley, 2019) identifies 8 main issues design thinking today:

Issue 1: the “image problem”.

This is mostly due to inexperienced designers who feel they are ‘experts’ because they understand the basic principles of design thinking. This leads to sub-par designs being fed to users, created through a clinical and ineffective process. Design thinking is not a cut and dry method that allows you to bypass the human engagement aspect of research. The fix? Focus on learning what works for users and seek out the advice of experienced designers before jumping into a design thinking approach.

Issue 2 & 3: assuming design thinking is "linear"

Some will try to treat design thinking as something which can be perfected in a matter of hours and will lead to outstanding results. Realistically, design thinking is a learned skill which takes years of practice to implement successfully. The fix? Set realistic expectations on how long it will take to find a solution. Most problems take between 4-8 weeks to solve and acknowledging this early avoids the realisation halfway through that you have wasted time trying to rush a solution.

Issue 4: wanting human-centric outputs without “human inputs”.

Designing for people is impossible without their input, unsurprisingly. Taking shortcuts past the critical stages of market research will only lead to you designing in a way that only pleases you (and it is very rare that, as a designer, you alone will be your sole target consumer). The only fix for this issue is to resist the urge to rush ahead. Shortcuts lead to shortcomings and the only way to know what people want is to ask them.

Issue 5: “misalignment” is a risk to every design thinking project.

Many projects run into problems because they fail to maintain alignment throughout the project. Design Thinking experts naturally spend more time with the client’s customers and digesting the findings to identify insights than anyone else involved with the project, leading to miscommunications within teams. This can be resolved by either creating a team with your client so they are aware of what you’re doing all of the time, or by making more time to communicate your work 100% effectively.

Issue 6: refusing to “re-frame” the problem.

It can be tempting to justify sticking with your first idea because your ‘gut knows best’, but the first solution is rarely the final one. Accepting criticism and taking time away from your project is the best solution. This is equally important for clients. Encouraging clients to reconsider their outlook is one of the most effective skills you can have as a designer. If they choose not to reframe their view, you have to find a solution that satisfies what they want from the project whilst still making sense.

Issue 7: using design thinking on “obvious” challenges.

Not every task you are given as a designer will warrant an innovative new idea. Sometimes, we do not properly consider the nature of the problem at hand and create overcomplicated solutions to basic problems. The best way to avoid this is to get to the root of the problem first. Figure out what caused the problem, what solution it requires then decide what the best method is.

Issue 8: design thinking is “marginalised”.

Design Thinking is not always understood and so is only approved for use at lower levels of an organisation. Stuck in the ‘basement’, even great solutions will have a tough time getting noticed. This can be due to an ignorant, ‘if it’s not broke, don’t fix it’ mentality. Some will not be receptive to new methods of research- especially when they require potentially time-consuming steps. Negotiating and involving sceptical clients in your design thinking research is the best way to remedy this issue.

Conclusion

IDEO shows us that design thinking is a hugely important tool for creatives in any industry. Design thinking allows us to interact with our client base in a more interactive way, whilst keeping the information digestible and clear. We can use design thinking to ensure the accessibility of our projects, to address environmental concerns surrounding digital products or to try and discover new problems from users. Design thinking can be an extremely effective way of innovating and avoiding the beaten path when it comes to designing.

However, design thinking is not perfect. Many issues such as inexperienced designers, the unwillingness of clients to try design thinking, a misunderstanding of the workload and the overuse of design thinking have created an uneasiness with some designers wherein they feel unable to propose a design thinking approach without sounding inexperienced.
